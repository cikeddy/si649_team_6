With this visualization analysis we endeavor to look at certain factors of healthcare in the US and how it compares to some of the other “comparable” countries. We used to OECD data set to explore this overarching question.

1. Which country spends the most on Health Care in our data set?

Based on the trend over time, you can see that in total the U.S. spent the most on healthcare compared to other countries from 1995 to 2018.

Using data from 2015, and numbers from the United States as a baseline for comparison (mid-line) the U.S. was second for household expenditure (the amount of money citizens pay out of pocket for their healthcare). We wanted to see if high healthcare expenditure resulted in high healthcare quality results.

2. Narrowing down our data set

There are more than 80 countries in the OECD dataset that we can compare. After plotting the countries’ health expenditures, we felt that there were too many components in a chart to compare. As a result, we thought it would be best to narrow our scope to 6-8 comparable countries based on a country’s GDP, per-capita GDP, and population. We applied the following criteria to select our comparable countries:

  1. OECD Countries: We selected OECD countries only, which reduced the total number of countries to 35.
  2. Economic Status: Divide remaining countries into two categories of High income and High-Medium income to choose from.
  3. High: GDP > $2 trillion; per-capita income > $30k; population > 60m
  4. High-Medium: $750 billion < GDP < $2 trillion; per-capita income > $30k; 15m < population < 60m
  5. Data Availability: Since there is a lot of missing data, at this step we chose to select only those countries with the most complete data.

Based on the criteria mentioned earlier, we decided to select the following seven comparable countries: United States, Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Great Britain and Netherlands.

3. What do the Quality attributes say?

A larger percentage of Americans believe their health is above "fair" than the other 6 countries. Based on this statistic and the money we spend on healthcare, we expected other quality metrics to align.

The United States has consistently had the lowest life expectancy at birth compared to the other 6 countries for the past 17 years.

The United States is also the only country that has had no growth in the number of primary care physicians (PCPs) over the past 18 years despite its population continuing to grow.

The United States also has the highest treatable mortality compared to the other 6 countries.

In addition, the United States is the only country with less than 99% of its population insured for healthcare.

4. Is it worth it to spend more?

Interestingly, countries with households that spend more on healthcare appear to have higher treatable mortality (deaths per 100,000 considered treatable through more effective and timely health care interventions), indicating that spending could be positively correlated with mortality.

5. So, where would you want to live?

To answer this question we calculated a return on investment (ROI) metric based on treatable morality and household spending ((100,000 - treatable mortality deaths)/(household spending)). Based on our calculation, France ranked #1 and the United States ranked last. You can explore each country's trend over time by clicking on it in the scatter chart on the left.
