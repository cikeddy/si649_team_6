2. Narrowing down our data set

There are more than 80 countries in the OECD dataset and after plotting their health expenditures, we felt there were too many components in our visualizatons (many of which might not be comparable). As a result, we thought it would be best to narrow our scope to 6-8 comparable countries based on a country’s GDP, per-capita GDP, and population. We applied the following criteria to select our final set of countries:

  1. OECD Countries: We selected OECD countries only, which reduced the total number of countries to 35.
  2. Economic Status: Divide remaining countries into two categories of High income and High-Medium income to choose from.
    1. High: GDP > $2 trillion; per-capita income > $30 thousand; population > 60 million
    2. High-Medium: $750 billion < GDP < $2 trillion; per-capita income > $30 thousand; 15 million < population < 60 million
  3. Data Availability: Since there is a lot of missing data, we chose to select only those countries with the most complete records.

3. What do the Quality attributes say?

The U.S. Has the Highest Percieved Health


Higher is Better

The vast majority of Americans have believed their health is above "fair" over the past 10 years. The United States has consistely ranked in the top 2 among comparable countries and, in 2015, ranked highest for this quality metric. Based on this statistic and the money we spend on healthcare, we expected other quality metrics to align.

The U.S. Has the Worst Life Expectancy


Lower is Worse

Surprisingly, the United States has consistently had the lowest life expectancy at birth compared to the other 6 countries for the past 15 years.

The U.S. Has the Lowest Number of Primary Care Physicians per 1,000 People


Lower is Worse

Again, the United States performs poorly for health care capacity; it is one of the only countries that has had no growth in the number of primary care physicians (PCPs) over the past 15 years.

The U.S. Has the Highest Treatable Mortality


Higher is Worse

The United States has also had the highest treatable mortality (deaths per 100,000 considered treatable through more effective and timely health care interventions) compared to the other 6 countries over the past 16 years.

5. So, where would you want to live?

To answer this question we calculated a return on investment (ROI) metric based on treatable mortality and household spending (100,000 - treatable mortality deaths)/(household spending). Based on our calculation, France ranked #1 and the United States ranked last.
